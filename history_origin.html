I. The Heart of Culture

Famous for the Moriones Festival, Marinduque is a living museum of faith and folklore. For over 200 years, the island has reenacted the story of Longinus, the Roman centurion, through a unique week-long street drama.

II. The Legend of Mariin & Duque

The Forbidden Love

Princess Maring, daughter of a strict datu, fell in love with Duque, a humble man who introduced farming to the people of Mount Malindig. Their love was forbidden due to their social status.

The Race & Tragedy

To find a husband, the Datu held a boat race. Wealthy suitors joined, but a violent storm—believed to be sent by Bathala—struck the fleet, proving the suitors' unworthiness.

The Birth of Marinduque

Realizing his mistake, the Datu blessed their union. The island was named Marinduque (Maring + Duque) in their honor. Some say the Tres Reyes Islands are the remains of the suitors' sunken ships.

III. Historical Timeline

920 – 1571

Early Settlers & Trade

Originally inhabited by Mangyan and Malay peoples, the island was a hub for Chinese traders during the Sung Dynasty. In 1571, Spanish explorers Martin de Goiti and Juan de Salcedo arrived, evolving the name from Malandic to Marinduque.

Spanish Colonial Era

Faith & Fortresses

Boac became the capital, housing a fortress-like church built in 1792 to protect locals from pirates. The Tres Reyes islands—Gaspar, Melchor, and Baltazar—stand as sentinels named after the Three Kings.

1901 – 1945

Resistance & Revolution

Marinduque was briefly independent in 1901. During the Phil-Am War, it saw the famous Battle of Pulang Lupa, where Filipino forces defeated American troops. It was later occupied by Japan and liberated in 1945.

Archaeology

Ancient Discoveries

In 1881, Antoine-Alfred Marche began excavations, finding 15th-century Chinese ceramics and gold ornaments, proving Marinduque's significance in pre-colonial trade networks.
