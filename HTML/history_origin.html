Overview

This small heart-shaped island is famous for its colorful Moriones Festival, a unique reenactment of a Roman centurion’s conversion to Christianity and his execution. Celebrated every Holy Week for over 200 years, it features masked men dressed as Roman soldiers.

The Forbidden Love

Princess Maring, daughter of a strict datu, often wandered Mount Malindig where she met Duque, a kind man who introduced farming. Despite social differences, they fell in love.

The Suitors and the Race

The datu organized a boat race to find a husband for Maring. Wealthy suitors joined, but Duque could not compete due to his status.

A Tragic Twist

A violent storm struck during the race, sinking ships. Some say Bathala caused it to stop the unjust marriage.

Birth of the Island

Realizing his mistake, the datu allowed Maring to marry Duque. The island was named Marinduque in their honor.

Alternative Origins

Another version links the name to “malindig,” meaning tall and elegant, referring to Mount Malindig. The Tres Reyes islands are said to be the sunken ships of the suitors.

Moriones Festival

The Moriones Festival is one of the oldest religious festivals in the Philippines, celebrated annually during Holy Week with colorful costumes and dramatic reenactments.

Early History

Marinduque’s earliest settlers were Mangyan and Malay peoples. Trade with Chinese merchants dates back to the Sung dynasty (920–1289).

Tres Reyes Islands

The Tres Reyes Islands—Gaspar, Melchor, and Baltazar—are named after the biblical Three Kings. Artifacts from a 15th-century Chinese shipwreck have been recovered in this area.

Spanish Period

Spanish explorers Martin de Goiti and Juan de Salcedo arrived in 1571 and claimed the island. The name evolved from “Malandic,” meaning sloping, to Marinduque.

Boac

Boac, the capital, is known for its historic church built in 1792. The fortress-like structure features thick walls and Filipino-Hispanic Gothic architecture.

Santa Cruz & Bathala Caves

Santa Cruz features a Spanish-era church and the Bathala Caves, a limestone cave system with religious formations, underground rivers, and historical remains.

American Period

Marinduque was briefly an independent province in 1901 before becoming part of Tayabas. It was reestablished as a separate province in 1920.

War History

During the Philippine-American War, Marinduque hosted concentration camps and the Battle of Pulang Lupa, where Filipino forces defeated American troops. It was later occupied by Japan in World War II and liberated in 1945.

Archaeology

Archaeological work began in Marinduque in 1881 by Antoine-Alfred Marche. Discoveries included Chinese ceramics, gold ornaments, and pre-colonial artifacts now housed in museums in France and the United States.
